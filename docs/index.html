Fiction as prophecy

When 1984 was published, it was received as dystopian fiction, but what Orwell didn't know he created was not just another narrative; it was a warning that has continued to resonate over time.

The Party's three slogans each describe a different axis of control. War is Peace kept populations docile through manufactured crisis. Freedom is Slavery reframed submission as liberation, making resistance feel like the real prison. But Ignorance is Strength is the one that does not require soldiers or surveillance to sustain itself.

That is the slogan this project follows into the data. The Party's deepest instrument was not the telescreen but the Ministry of Truth, which did not merely censor history but rewrote it, quietly and bureaucratically, until certain ideas could no longer be formed at all. The modern form of that mechanism appears in budget cuts and school board directives, in administrators pulling books from library shelves rather than burning them. Where regimes once manufactured obedient soldiers, they now manufacture incurious citizens, people who do not resist power because they have never been given the language to name it.

"The Party told you to reject the evidence of your eyes and ears. It was their final, most essential command."

George Orwell, 1984
Global Context · 1990–2025

A World Without Academic Freedom

Seventy-five years later, political scientists have built a framework that measures exactly what Orwell described, not in Oceania, but in 179 real countries.

The Varieties of Democracy (V-Dem) Institute's Academic Freedom Index scores each country from 0 (total suppression) to 1 (full freedom), encoded in colour: blue is freedom, red is suppression, and no shade is neutral (Spannagel & Kinzelbach, 2023). This animation runs from 1990 to 2025; press play.

The early 1990s show Eastern Europe opening after Soviet collapse. After 2010, Hungary and Poland begin reddening inside the EU. Turkey drops sharply after 2016, when over 6,000 academics were dismissed under emergency decrees (Human Rights Watch, 2018; Middle East Research and Information Project, 2018), and Russia consolidates following Putin's 2012 return and the foreign agent laws that followed (PONARS Eurasia, 2022).

The most striking shift belongs to the United States, whose score held near the top of the scale for decades before falling around 2016 and accelerating sharply after 2021. By 2025, the US AFI has dropped to levels associated with countries undergoing active democratic backsliding, encoding not a regime change but a measurable erosion of the conditions under which knowledge is produced and taught.

The Precondition · 1900–2025

Academic Freedom Requires Democracy to Exist

Before the average person had access to today’s vast ocean of data, Orwell had already anticipated this shift. In 1984, the Party's control over Oceania rested on a single mechanism: controlling what can be thought. The Ministry of Truth did not merely censor, it adjusted history, science was bended to ideology, and the academic class became an instrument of the state rather than a check on it. Our reality reflects this, the data confirms it.

Across 35 years of data, the pattern barely changes: countries with less political freedom also have less academic freedom. Closed autocracies consistently cluster at the bottom of the chart, while liberal democracies remain at the top, forming four stable regime groups across every year in the dataset. The point is not simply that dictatorships score lower on democracy measures, but that restricting political freedom reliably erodes academic freedom as well.

Orwell called it Newspeak, a language engineered to make dissent literally unthinkable. The regimes in the bottom-left of this chart can't just burn books, this doesn’t get to the root of “issue”, what they need, is to also control who gets to write them.

Historical Pattern · 1900–2025

Democracy Is the Exception, Not the Rule

The correlation between academic freedom and regime type is not merely an academic curiosity; it is a warning written across the full sweep of modern history.

Closed autocracy has been the default condition of human governance since 1900, and the democratic expansion that followed the Second World War was a remarkable, fragile exception rather than an inevitability. Since 2010, the share of liberal democracies has fallen from 25% to 17% of all nations, and by 2025, for the first time in over two decades, there are more autocracies in the world than democracies.

The consequence for intellectual life is direct and measurable: as political freedom contracts, academic freedom contracts with it, consistently and across countries, not only in extreme cases. The pattern has appeared across enough countries and enough decades that its early signatures have become recognisable, and some of those signatures are now appearing in places that would have seemed unthinkable a decade ago.

Whether the United States is at the beginning of that trajectory, or experiencing a turbulent moment that will self-correct, remains genuinely contested. The data offers one uncomfortable reminder: in the countries that now sit in the bottom-left of this chart, there was almost always a period when the question still felt open.

Case Studies · 2010–2025

Different Paths, One Destination

Four countries are chosen to represent different trajectories: two historical totalitarianisms, one rising authoritarian state, and one contemporary democracy under stress.

Every single one has declined in academic freedom index since 2010.

Case Study: Russia · The Textbook

Putin's Ministry of Truth

In August 2023, Russia introduced a single mandatory state history textbook for all high school students which was the first unified national textbook since the collapse of the USSR, according to its co-author, presidential aide Vladimir Medinsky (RBC, 2023). It completely rewrites every section from the 1970s onward, and adds an entirely new chapter on the war in Ukraine.

That chapter teaches that Western powers conspired to destabilise Russia, that Ukraine was deliberately cultivated as an anti-Russian state over generations, and that the invasion is an act of defense – not aggression. Ukrainian identity itself is framed as an artificial construct engineered by Austria in the 19th century. Students are instructed to be suspicious of any online information about Ukraine, warned that "the global industry for the production of staged videos, stuffing, fake photos and videos works as a continuous conveyor belt" (RBC, 2023).

The Education Minister confirmed the textbook was produced in five months, and pledged it would be updated again... "after our victory."

650,000 students received it in September 2023.

Case Study: China · The Curriculum

Shaped Before the Question Forms

In September 2021, China's Ministry of Education began incorporating Xi Jinping's political ideology into the national curriculum at every level of schooling from primary school through university. Teachers were instructed by government notice to plant "the seeds of loving the party, the country and socialism in young hearts" (Al Jazeera, 2021).

China researcher Adam Ni argued that the policy was less about nationalism than about promoting the cult of Xi, a personality cult that independent observers drew directly to Mao, not as criticism, but as a blueprint consciously revisited (as cited in Al Jazeera, 2021).

Case Study: Hungary · The Legal Playbook

Making It Too Expensive to Stay

Orbán's playbook was surgical, he didn't shut down all universities instead he targeted one. In April 2017, the Hungarian parliament passed a law, in under a week of deliberation, imposing conditions on foreign universities that Human Rights Watch (2017) described as appearing "directed at CEU specifically."

Central European University, founded in 1991 as a symbol of post-Soviet democratic renewal, with graduates including several members of Orbán's own government, was required to establish a US campus, submit to bilateral government contracts, and operate under a different name. The practical effect was to make its continued operation in Hungary impossible. CEU eventually relocated to Vienna.

Human Rights Watch researcher Lydia Gall noted at the time that the law "seems motivated by a desire to silence critical voices in Hungary" (as cited in Human Rights Watch, 2017). It worked and every tactic used such as defunding through legal mechanism, demanding institutional reorganisation, framing foreign influence as a threat, has since reappeared in the United States.

Case Study: United States · The Present

The United States Is Not Watching from the Sidelines

0.908 → 0.397

Between 2024 and 2025 alone, America's institutional autonomy score fell from 2.4 to 1.7 on a 4-point scale, what Kinzelbach et al. (2026) describe as "fast and steep deterioration," driven by "an unprecedented array of coercive federal executive measures."

DOGE cuts left the Department of Education with 1,700 fewer employees and cancelled millions in federal research dollars (Brookings Institution, 2026). Columbia University lost $400 million in federal funding after the White House demanded it reorganise its Middle East studies department and revise protest policies, with explicit warnings that similar consequences awaited other universities (US News, 2025).

The ideological groundwork had been laid in advance. Project 2025, the Heritage Foundation's governing blueprint, called for the removal of critical race theory and gender ideology from curricula and framed higher education as having been "captured by woke diversicrats" (PEN America, 2024). That language became executive action within months of the administration taking office.

What is unfolding is less a culture war than a resource war. The most effective way to reshape what universities teach, research, and tolerate is not to ban ideas outright but to make the institutions that house them financially dependent on those who find those ideas threatening.

US Education · July 2021 – June 2022

The Same Logic Operating One Level Down

The pressure described in the previous slide targets universities, but the same structural logic has been applied simultaneously to public schools. While federal funding was being weaponised against higher education, state legislatures were passing laws that removed books from school libraries at a scale not seen in modern American history.

In a single academic year spanning July 2021 to June 2022, 2,532 books were banned from US public school libraries and classrooms across 32 states and 138 districts (PEN America, 2022). The distribution across the map is not uniform: two states alone account for more than half of all bans, and their concentration reflects the geography of legislation rather than any spontaneous community action.

Among the titles removed was George Orwell's Nineteen Eighty-Four. An Iowa law passed in 2023 triggered removals broad enough to include a novel written as a direct warning against exactly this kind of state-directed information control. A federal judge blocked the law in March 2025 before it could take full effect (The Guardian, 2025), but the passage of the law itself was already the signal.

Hover any state to see its ban count alongside its 2020 presidential vote share.

US Book Bans · When

A Policy Spike, Not a Cultural Shift

This chart shows monthly ban counts across the 2021 to 2022 academic year. In July 2021, twelve books were banned. In August 2021, that number jumped to 443, with no preceding public debate, no visible shift in community attitudes, and no gradual escalation to explain it. The pattern continued through the end of the year, peaking at 513 bans in December 2021 before tapering as the academic year closed.

The timing corresponds precisely with the implementation of state legislation passed in the preceding months. PEN America (2023) documented that the surge was directly driven by new state laws requiring districts to audit and remove materials, with Florida and Texas accounting for the largest share of bans. A community does not spontaneously remove hundreds of books from school shelves in a single month across dozens of districts simultaneously. That kind of synchronised volume is the signature of a policy directive reaching institutions at the same moment.

Federal Spending · FY2017–2026

The Spending Chart Is the Argument Made Visible

The case studies above describe how coercion works at the level of individual institutions. This chart shows what it looks like in aggregate, across every quarter of federal education spending from 2017 to 2026.

The Biden years produced the highest quarterly obligations on record, peaking above $38 billion in early 2021 as pandemic recovery funds flowed through the system. What follows in the Trump second term is not a return to pre-Biden norms. Department of Education quarterly obligations contracted by 30 to 31 percent in mid-year quarters, with Q3 2026 recording $1.57 billion, the lowest non-zero figure in the entire dataset (USASpending.gov, 2026).

The resource war described in the United States case study is not an abstraction. It is the difference between the green bars and the red ones, rendered in dollars, quarter by quarter.

The Long Run · Literacy & Democracy

Literate, Incurious, Compliant

This chart replaces academic freedom on the y-axis with a simpler measure: adult literacy. The four regime clusters remain, the diagonal pattern persists, and across 25 years of animation, very little changes. That stability is part of the point.

The median literacy gap between closed autocracies and liberal democracies is 44 percentage points, a gap that developed over decades through education funding, curriculum design, and the gradual narrowing of what teachers are permitted to teach. Regime type is not the only driver of literacy outcomes, but across every year in this dataset it is among the most consistent ones.

Russia, China, Hungary, and the United States all sit above 95% literacy. That number is not reassuring. Ampleforth, in 1984, was a genuinely skilled poet who spent his days perfecting Newspeak verse. He was not a warning about ignorance but about intelligence aimed at everything except the right question.

What the data shows is not a decline in literacy but a narrowing of what literacy is for. The books removed, the universities defunded, the history rewritten: none of it will lower a test score. It will simply ensure that the test is the only thing the score was ever good for.
